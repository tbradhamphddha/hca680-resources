P · S · D · R · C

High Reliability Organizations create a mindfulness infrastructure through five interconnected principles. Mindfulness is a rich awareness and a capacity for action that facilitates discovering and managing unexpected events before they escalate into crises. The five principles below use the PSDRC format. Click each principle to see what it looks like in practice.

P

Preoccupation with Failure

Finding and regarding small, inconsequential errors as a symptom that something is wrong; finding the half event.
HRO in Action
  • When you treat a near miss as a learning opportunity rather than a lucky break, you are practicing preoccupation with failure.
  • When you report a close call even though no one was harmed, you are practicing preoccupation with failure.
  • When you ask "what can go wrong?" before starting a procedure, you are practicing preoccupation with failure.
  • When you update a procedure after experiencing a close call, you are practicing preoccupation with failure.
  • When you reward someone for spotting a problem or near miss, you are practicing preoccupation with failure.
  • When you seek out and encourage bad news from your team, you are practicing preoccupation with failure.
  • When you focus on the work system rather than blaming the person, you are practicing preoccupation with failure.
  • When you create an environment where people feel free to talk to leaders about problems, you are practicing preoccupation with failure.
What Can I Do?
  • Be on the lookout for small, emerging failures.
  • Take small failures and near misses seriously. Fix small problems before they grow into big ones.
  • Focus on the work system or work process, not the person.
R

Reluctance to Simplify Interpretations

Thinking critically and encouraging diversity in experience, perspective, and opinion.
HRO in Action
  • When you question an assumption rather than accepting it at face value, you are practicing reluctance to simplify interpretations.
  • When you ask "why?" five times to dig deeper into a complex problem, you are practicing reluctance to simplify interpretations.
  • When you encourage a colleague to share a different perspective, you are practicing reluctance to simplify interpretations.
  • When you challenge the status quo instead of accepting "that's the way we've always done it," you are practicing reluctance to simplify interpretations.
  • When you spend more time analyzing an unexpected event than defending your initial view, you are practicing reluctance to simplify interpretations.
  • When you value the skeptic on the team for raising uncomfortable questions, you are practicing reluctance to simplify interpretations.
  • When you listen carefully to ensure no one's view goes unheard, you are practicing reluctance to simplify interpretations.
  • When you look for incomplete information or assumptions masquerading as facts, you are practicing reluctance to simplify interpretations.
What Can I Do?
  • Be on the lookout for incorrect, incomplete information or assumptions masquerading as facts.
  • Think using a Questioning Mind to break confirmation bias. Confirmation bias (stopping when a fact supports your conclusion) defeats this principle.
  • Do not accept simple explanations for complex problems. Ask why five times to dig deeper.
S

Sensitivity to Operations

Situational awareness: paying attention to what is happening on the front line. Seeing the right work done the right way.
HRO in Action
  • When you think ahead to your needs for the day, you are being sensitive to operations.
  • When you think about how your work affects others, you are being sensitive to operations.
  • When you offer to help other teams, you are being sensitive to operations.
  • When you ask questions during a tiered huddle, you are being sensitive to operations.
  • When you make sure people have what they need to do their job, you are being sensitive to operations.
  • When you gather your team to discuss the day, you are being sensitive to operations.
  • When you check to see how things are going, you are being sensitive to operations.
  • When you focus on how work is actually done rather than how it was designed, you are being sensitive to operations.
What Can I Do?
  • Maintain situational awareness of your work. Share that awareness often with the team.
  • Speak up during tiered huddles. Share your concerns and needs. Identify impacts your work has on others.
  • Focus on how the work is actually done regardless of how work is imagined or designed.
D

Deference to Expertise

Pushing decision making down and around to the person with the most related knowledge and expertise, regardless of rank.
HRO in Action
  • When you ask for help or advice or support, you are practicing deference to expertise.
  • When you include patient and family in care choices, you are practicing deference to expertise.
  • When you stop to get help, you are practicing deference to expertise.
  • When you read and acknowledge a best practice alert, you are practicing deference to expertise.
  • When you stop and check the policy or protocol, you are practicing deference to expertise.
  • When someone says you should stop and you do, and you thank them, you are practicing deference to expertise.
  • When you make the space safe for others to ask questions, you are practicing deference to expertise.
  • When you support someone who asks a question or asks to stop, you are practicing deference to expertise.
What Can I Do?
  • Encourage a culture of teamwork and shared decision-making.
  • Ask others for their thoughts. Offer your thoughts when asked.
  • Practice the social skills that flatten authority gradients and reduce power distance.
C

Commitment to Resilience

Developing capabilities to detect, contain, and bounce back from upsets and events that do occur.
HRO in Action
  • When you participate in training and simulation exercises, you are demonstrating commitment to resilience.
  • When you ask "what if...?" as a normal part of your work, you are demonstrating commitment to resilience.
  • When you learn from mistakes and share those lessons with others, you are demonstrating commitment to resilience.
  • When you take on challenging stretch assignments to build new skills, you are demonstrating commitment to resilience.
  • When you use your knowledge in novel ways to solve an unfamiliar problem, you are demonstrating commitment to resilience.
  • When you build informal networks of contacts you can call on to solve problems, you are demonstrating commitment to resilience.
  • When you rely on one another during a crisis rather than working in isolation, you are demonstrating commitment to resilience.
  • When you invest in developing your team's competence and response repertoires, you are demonstrating commitment to resilience.
What Can I Do?
  • Mentally simulate your work: what activities lie upstream and downstream? How can things unravel?
  • Build capabilities to cope with disturbances through varied job experiences and cross-training.
  • Encourage flexibility and remove barriers to cross-functional collaboration on your team.
