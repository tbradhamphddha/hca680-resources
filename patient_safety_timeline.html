Era 1: The wake-up call (1945 to 2001)

Accreditation infrastructure predates the safety movement by decades. Then landmark research, nursing home scandals, and a single report changed everything.

1945-79First accreditation bodies established across the care continuumStandards
The accreditation infrastructure was built decades before the patient safety movement:
HFAP (1945): First U.S. hospital accreditor, operated by the American Osteopathic Association. Merged with ACHC in 2020.
TJC (1951): Joint Commission on Accreditation of Hospitals, formed by merging the ACS Hospital Standardization Program with ACP, AHA, and AMA programs. Began accrediting hospitals in 1953.
CAP (1962): College of American Pathologists began laboratory accreditation using a peer-inspection model. Holds CLIA deeming authority.
CHAP (1965): Community Health Accreditation Partner. Accredits home health agencies and hospices. CMS deeming authority for home health and hospice.
CARF (1966): Commission on Accreditation of Rehabilitation Facilities. Accredits rehabilitation and behavioral health programs. Not direct CMS deeming, but tied to state Medicaid payment in many states.
COA (1977): Council on Accreditation. Focuses on social service and behavioral health organizations.
AAAHC (1979): Accreditation Association for Ambulatory Health Care. Focuses on ambulatory surgery centers, primary care, and outpatient settings. CMS deeming authority for ASCs granted 1996.
NCQA (1979): National Committee for Quality Assurance. Focuses on health plan quality. Began developing HEDIS in 1989 (Version 1.0 released 1991). Over 90% of U.S. health plans use HEDIS.

1965Medicare/Medicaid: Accreditation linked to paymentFederal
The Social Security Amendments of 1965 created Medicare and Medicaid and established that hospitals accredited by the Joint Commission were deemed to meet Medicare Conditions of Participation. This was the foundational federal decision linking accreditation to payment, creating the "deeming authority" framework that still governs healthcare accreditation today. TJC's standards literally became the original Conditions of Participation.

1984Harvard Medical Practice StudyResearch
Landmark study of 30,000+ hospitalizations in New York found adverse events in 3.7% of admissions, with 27.6% due to negligence. Published in 1991, this became the empirical foundation for To Err Is Human. Led by Lucian Leape, Troyen Brennan, and others.
Click to expand ↓
1987Nursing Home Reform Act (OBRA '87)Federal
Following an IOM investigation into widespread nursing home abuse and neglect, Congress enacted the first major reform of long-term care standards since 1965. OBRA '87 established minimum care standards, a Residents' Bill of Rights, prohibited unnecessary physical and chemical restraints, required comprehensive resident assessments (Minimum Data Set), and standardized the survey and enforcement system. Physical restraint use dropped from 37% to 28% in the first years. This legislation remains the foundation for nursing home regulation today.
Click to expand ↓
1991HEDIS measurement begins (Version 1.0)Standards
NCQA released the first Healthcare Effectiveness Data and Information Set, originally called the HMO Employer Data and Information Set. Created the first standardized performance measures for health plans across effectiveness of care, access, and patient experience. Today, more than 235 million people are enrolled in plans that report HEDIS results across 90+ measures in 6 domains. CMS requires Medicare Advantage HMOs to submit HEDIS data.
Click to expand ↓
1994Lucian Leape: "Error in Medicine" published in JAMAResearch
Leape argued that medicine should adopt the systems-based error prevention approaches used in aviation and other high-risk industries. A radical reframing away from individual blame toward system design.
Click to expand ↓
1997National Patient Safety Foundation (NPSF) establishedIndustry
Grew out of a 1996 conference organized by the AAAS, AMA, and the Annenberg Center for Health Sciences, funded by the Robert Wood Johnson Foundation. Martin Hatlie (AMA legal counsel) was the driving force, modeling NPSF on the Anesthesia Patient Safety Foundation (APSF) founded by Jeep Pierce and Jeff Cooper. AMA Board Chair Nancy Dickey championed the initiative through seven months of Board deliberations. Key founding participants: Richard Cook (U of Chicago), Jeffrey Cooper (MGH), David Gaba (Stanford), David Woods (Ohio State), and Lucian Leape (Harvard). Initial funding from AMA ($1M), 3M ($1M), CNA HealthPro ($1M), and Schering-Plough. Diane Pinakiewicz became president when NPSF separated from the AMA in 2004. Merged with IHI in 2017.
Click to expand ↓
1999IOM: To Err Is HumanResearch
The Institute of Medicine reported 44,000 to 98,000 Americans die annually from preventable medical errors in hospitals, more than car accidents, breast cancer, or AIDS. Called for a 50% reduction in errors within 5 years and proposed a national patient safety infrastructure. This single report launched the modern patient safety movement.
Click to expand ↓
1999Healthcare Research and Quality ActFederal
Signed into law weeks after To Err Is Human. Designated AHRQ as the federal lead agency for patient safety research. Charged AHRQ with identifying causes of preventable errors, developing strategies to reduce them, and disseminating effective solutions.
Click to expand ↓
2000AHRQ: Doing What Counts for Patient SafetyResponse
Released just 60 days after To Err Is Human, this AHRQ-led federal task force report outlined specific strategies to curb medical errors, including developing Patient Safety Indicators and funding safety research.
Click to expand ↓
2001IOM: Crossing the Quality ChasmResearch
The IOM's follow-up broadened the conversation beyond errors to full quality of care, establishing six aims: safe, effective, patient-centered, timely, efficient, and equitable. This framework shaped quality and safety work for the next two decades.

Era 2: Building infrastructure (2002 to 2009)

The movement shifted from awareness to action: reporting systems, safety goals, national campaigns, federal protections, and new accreditors across the care continuum.

2002NQF: Serious Reportable Events ("Never Events")Standards
The National Quality Forum published its first list of 27 serious reportable events that should never occur (wrong-site surgery, retained foreign objects, patient death from falls). Became the foundation for CMS non-payment policies. Updated in 2006, 2011, now listing 29 events.
Click to expand ↓
2003Joint Commission: First National Patient Safety GoalsStandards
TJC leveraged its accreditation authority to identify required steps for preventing errors. NPSGs covered patient identification, communication, medication safety, infection prevention, and fall reduction. Updated annually through 2025, then replaced by National Performance Goals in 2026.
Click to expand ↓
2004IHI: 100,000 Lives CampaignResponse
Led by Donald Berwick, IHI challenged U.S. hospitals to adopt six evidence-based interventions to prevent avoidable deaths. Over 3,000 hospitals participated. Estimated 122,000+ lives saved in 18 months. Followed by the 5 Million Lives Campaign (2006 to 2008) expanding to 12 interventions.
Click to expand ↓
2005Patient Safety and Quality Improvement Act (PSQIA)Federal
Created Patient Safety Organizations (PSOs) and provided federal privilege and confidentiality protections for voluntarily reported patient safety information ("patient safety work product"). Providers could now report and analyze errors without fear of increased liability, enabling honest safety culture. AHRQ oversees PSO listing; OCR enforces confidentiality provisions.
Click to expand ↓
2006WHO: Surgical Safety Checklist development beginsResponse
WHO launched a global effort culminating in the Surgical Safety Checklist (published 2008), based on Atul Gawande's research. Studies showed the checklist reduced surgical deaths by 47% and complications by 36%. Adopted by thousands of hospitals worldwide.
Click to expand ↓
2008CMS: Hospital-Acquired Conditions non-payment policyFederal
CMS stopped paying hospitals for certain preventable conditions acquired during hospitalization (CAUTI, CLABSI, falls, wrong-site surgery). First time the federal government used payment policy as a direct lever for patient safety, based on the NQF "Never Events" framework. Safety became a financial imperative, not just a moral one.
Click to expand ↓
2008-13CMS expands deeming authority across care settingsStandards
CMS broadened accreditation options beyond the original TJC/HFAP framework:
DNV Healthcare (2008): First new hospital accreditor since 1945. Integrates ISO 9001 quality management with Medicare CoPs. Uses NIAHO standards. Annual surveys.
AAAASF (est. 1980): American Association for Accreditation of Ambulatory Surgery Facilities. CMS deeming authority for ambulatory surgical centers.
ACHC (est. 1986): Accreditation Commission for Health Care. CMS deeming for home health, hospice. Merged with HFAP in 2020, gaining hospital deeming authority.
URAC (est. 1990): Utilization Review Accreditation Commission. Over 30 accreditation programs spanning health plans, pharmacy, digital health. CMS-approved for Medicare Advantage deeming.
CIHQ (2013): Center for Improvement in Healthcare Quality. Newest hospital accreditor. Standards most closely mirror CMS CoPs.
Also in 2008, the Medicare Improvements for Patients and Providers Act (MIPPA) removed TJC's statutorily guaranteed deeming authority, requiring it to apply through CMS like all other accreditors for the first time.
Click to expand ↓
2009HITECH Act (part of ARRA)Federal
The Health Information Technology for Economic and Clinical Health Act incentivized EHR adoption with $27 billion. Hospital EHR adoption accelerated from roughly 10% to over 95%. Key safety features: computerized physician order entry (CPOE), clinical decision support, and barcode medication administration.
Click to expand ↓
Era 3: Accountability and value (2010 to 2019)

The ACA embedded safety into payment policy across settings. The movement grappled with slow progress and expanded safety beyond the hospital to ambulatory, behavioral health, and long-term care.

2010Affordable Care Act: patient safety provisionsFederal
The ACA created multiple safety levers: Hospital-Acquired Condition Reduction Program (1% payment penalty for worst quartile), Hospital Value-Based Purchasing Program, Hospital Readmissions Reduction Program, and the Partnership for Patients initiative. The Nursing Home Transparency and Improvement Act (within the ACA) required public transparency of nursing home ownership, quality, and staffing. Safety became directly linked to reimbursement across settings.
Click to expand ↓
2011Partnership for Patients launchedResponse
CMS-led public-private partnership: reduce hospital-acquired conditions by 40% and readmissions by 20%. Over 3,700 hospitals participated through Hospital Engagement Networks. By 2014, HHS estimated 50,000 fewer patient deaths and $12 billion in savings.
Click to expand ↓
2012ABIM Foundation: Choosing Wisely campaignResponse
Launched to promote conversations between clinicians and patients about avoiding unnecessary tests, treatments, and procedures. Nine specialty societies initially offered 45 examples of commonly overused services. By 2023, 80+ societies contributed 700+ recommendations. Spread to 20+ countries. Addressed the patient safety dimension of overuse: up to 30% of U.S. healthcare spending goes toward unnecessary care that may cause harm.
Click to expand ↓
2012NCQA and URAC recognized as QHP accrediting entitiesStandards
HHS recognized NCQA and URAC as accrediting entities for Qualified Health Plans under the ACA. Health plans operating in the Affordable Insurance Exchanges (including federally facilitated and state partnership exchanges) must be accredited by a recognized entity. This extended accreditation requirements beyond hospitals to health plans as a condition of marketplace participation.
Click to expand ↓
2013James: Preventable deaths estimated at 210,000 to 440,000/yearResearch
John T. James published in the Journal of Patient Safety that original IOM estimates were significantly low. Using updated methodology, he estimated 210,000 to 440,000 patients per year experience preventable harm contributing to death. Reignited urgency and measurement debate.
Click to expand ↓
2014CMS: Behavioral health and psychiatric facility safety standardsFederal
CMS updated Conditions of Participation for psychiatric hospitals (42 CFR 482.60-62) and finalized requirements for the Inpatient Psychiatric Facility Quality Reporting (IPFQR) Program, including measures for hours of physical restraint use, seclusion, patients discharged on multiple antipsychotics, metabolic screening, and transition records. Rehabilitation facilities also received updated CoPs addressing patient safety.
Click to expand ↓
2014HHS: HACs reduced 17%, saving 87,000 lives (2010 to 2014)Response
AHRQ National Scorecard showed hospital-acquired conditions dropped 17%: 2.1 million fewer harms, $20 billion in savings, 87,000 lives saved. Demonstrated large-scale national efforts could produce results, though critics noted progress came mostly from targeted interventions (CLABSI, CAUTI) rather than systemic change.
Click to expand ↓
2015NAM: Improving Diagnosis in Health CareResearch
The National Academies expanded safety to include diagnostic errors, estimated to affect roughly 5% of U.S. adults in outpatient settings annually. Broadened patient safety beyond hospitals and beyond errors of commission to include errors of omission and cognition.
Click to expand ↓
2015NPSF: Free from Harm, 15-year progress reportResponse
NPSF concluded improvements occurred at a "much slower rate than initially expected." Recommended eight priorities including total systems safety, culture of safety, patient and family engagement, and workforce safety.
Click to expand ↓
2016CMS: Updated Nursing Home Requirements of ParticipationFederal
Most comprehensive revision to nursing home regulations since OBRA '87. Required compliance and ethics programs, antibiotic stewardship, updated infection control standards, expanded resident rights, discharge planning improvements, and QAPI programs in all long-term care facilities. Extended protections for residents in behavioral health and dementia care units.
Click to expand ↓
201621st Century Cures ActFederal
Addressed safety across multiple care settings: combating the opioid crisis (behavioral health safety), advancing EHR interoperability (reducing information gaps during care transitions), Helping Families in Mental Health Crisis provisions, expanding telehealth access, and information blocking provisions ensuring patients can access their health data across ambulatory, hospital, and behavioral health settings.
Click to expand ↓
2017NPSF merges with IHIIndustry
Consolidated the two leading safety and quality organizations. Signaled that safety and quality improvement were inseparable disciplines. Lucian Leape Institute continued within IHI.
Click to expand ↓
2018National Steering Committee for Patient Safety convenedResponse
IHI convened 27 organizations: patient advocates, provider organizations, federal agencies, and safety experts. The broadest collaborative in patient safety history. Their work culminated in Safer Together (2020).
Click to expand ↓
Era 4: Structural transformation (2020 to present)

COVID-19 reversed years of progress. The response: a shift from measuring outcomes to requiring safety infrastructure, with unprecedented mandates on culture, leadership, and transparency across the continuum.

2020IHI/NSC: Safer TogetherResponse
The 27-member National Steering Committee published 17 recommendations across four areas: culture/leadership/governance; patient and family engagement; workforce safety; and learning systems. Each PSSM domain maps directly to a Safer Together recommendation.
Click to expand ↓
2020-22COVID-19 reverses safety gainsResearch
CLABSI rates up 28%, CAUTI up 19%, MRSA up 14%, C. diff up 4% (vs. 2019). Nursing homes experienced devastating outbreaks. Demonstrated that safety systems were not robust enough to withstand crisis, exactly the vulnerability HRO principles are designed to address.
Click to expand ↓
2020ACHC merges with HFAPStandards
Accreditation Commission for Health Care and AAHHS (HFAP's parent) became the first two CMS-deemed accrediting organizations to merge, combining ACHC's home health and hospice expertise with HFAP's 75-year hospital accreditation legacy.
Click to expand ↓
2021WHO: Global Patient Safety Action Plan 2021 to 2030Industry
First global framework for patient safety: "a world in which no one is harmed in health care." Strategic objectives around policies, systems, clinical processes, and patient and family engagement. Endorsed by all WHO member states.
Click to expand ↓
2022NAM: The National Imperative to Improve Nursing Home QualityFederal
National Academies called for fundamental reform of nursing home care, regulation, and financing. Documented persistent quality problems despite 30+ years of OBRA '87. Recommended workforce investment, transparency, accountability, and a shift from minimum-standard compliance to continuous improvement.
Click to expand ↓
2022PCAST: Report on patient safety to the PresidentResearch
President's Council of Advisors on Science and Technology called for renewed federal commitment to safety. Noted progress had stalled and COVID-19 exposed fundamental weaknesses. Recommended structural measures, leadership accountability, and patient engagement. Directly influenced the CMS PSSM.
Click to expand ↓
2023CMS: First federal minimum staffing standards for nursing homesFederal
CMS proposed the first-ever federal minimum staffing requirements: 0.55 RN hours per resident day, 2.45 nurse aide hours per resident day, and an RN on-site 24/7. Linked to post-COVID evidence that inadequate staffing was the primary driver of poor long-term care outcomes. Finalized in 2024 with phased implementation.
Click to expand ↓
2024CMS: Patient Safety Structural Measure (PSSM) finalizedFederal
25 attestation statements across five domains: leadership commitment, strategic planning, culture of safety, accountability and transparency, patient and family engagement. First time CMS mandated reporting on safety infrastructure and culture rather than just outcomes. Hospitals report starting CY 2025; scores public on Care Compare fall 2026; non-reporters face reduced Medicare payments FY 2027.
Click to expand ↓
2025CMS: World Patient Safety Day commitment and new eCQMsFederal
On September 17, 2025, CMS committed to new initiatives including AI-supported digital quality measures. Expanded Hospital Harm eCQM set: falls with injury, postoperative respiratory failure, hypoglycemia/hyperglycemia. By 2026, seven Hospital Harm eCQMs are poised to replace traditional PSI claims measures across hospital, ambulatory, and outpatient settings.
Click to expand ↓
2026Joint Commission: NPGs + Accreditation 360Standards
Replaced 23 years of NPSGs with 14 National Performance Goals: task-based compliance to measurable outcomes. Introduced Accreditation 360 (continuous readiness vs. periodic surveys), SAFEST (spotlighting high-performing hospitals), and NPG 12 (staffing as a patient safety goal). Most significant accreditation overhaul since 1965.
Click to expand ↓
2026PSSM scores published on CMS Care CompareFederal
Beginning fall 2026, every acute care hospital's safety structure score (0 to 5) is publicly visible on Medicare.gov. Patients and families can see whether leadership, culture, and systems prioritize safety. Non-reporters face reduced Medicare payments FY 2027.

Key patterns for discussion

1. Crisis leads to legislation leads to infrastructure: Each era begins with a catalyzing event (research, pandemic, report) that generates political will, which creates infrastructure the safety movement builds upon.

2. Voluntary to mandatory: Practices that started as voluntary best practices (safety reporting, just culture, CRP, patient engagement) are progressively becoming regulatory requirements.

3. Outcomes to structure: Regulatory focus has shifted from measuring what went wrong (HAC rates, PSI scores, mortality) to requiring that the right systems exist to prevent harm (PSSM, Accreditation 360).

4. Individual to system: The conceptual frame evolved from individual blame (malpractice era) to systems thinking (To Err Is Human) to organizational culture and leadership accountability (PSSM, NPG 2).

5. Hospital to continuum: Safety regulation began in hospitals, expanded to nursing homes (OBRA '87), and now addresses ambulatory care (diagnostic errors, eCQMs), behavioral health (IPFQR, 21st Century Cures), long-term care (minimum staffing), home health, and care transitions.

6. Accreditation as infrastructure: Deeming authority created a powerful incentive structure: accreditation equals payment eligibility. As CMS expanded deeming across settings and broke TJC's monopoly (MIPPA 2008), market competition among accreditors introduced choice into quality oversight.

7. Persistent gap: Despite 25+ years of effort, preventable harm remains stubbornly high. COVID-19 demonstrated that safety gains were fragile, raising the question of whether structural mandates can create resilience that voluntary efforts could not.

Discussion questions

1. Why did it take from 1999 (To Err Is Human) to 2024 (PSSM) for the federal government to require hospitals to demonstrate safety infrastructure rather than just measure outcomes?

2. The PSQIA (2005) created confidentiality protections to encourage honest reporting. The PSSM (2024) requires transparency and public reporting. How do you reconcile these two approaches?

3. COVID-19 reversed years of HAC reduction progress. What does this tell us about the difference between high reliability and ordinary safety improvement?

4. If you were a hospital CEO in 2026, which developments on this timeline would most influence how you allocate resources to patient safety?

5. Compare the regulatory trajectory for hospitals with that for nursing homes, ambulatory care, and behavioral health settings. Where are the gaps?

6. MIPPA (2008) ended TJC's guaranteed deeming authority and opened the door for competing accreditors. Has competition improved safety oversight, or created a "race to the bottom"?
