Why CANDOR Matters

For decades, the default response when a patient was unexpectedly harmed followed a "deny and defend" strategy — restrict information, avoid admissions of fault, and let legal teams manage the fallout. This approach fails everyone: patients and families feel abandoned, providers carry guilt in silence, organizations miss opportunities to learn, and litigation increases rather than decreases.

Communication and Resolution Programs (CRPs) offer a fundamentally different path. Rather than circling the wagons, organizations commit to transparent communication with patients and families, thorough investigation of what went wrong, support for the providers involved, and fair resolution — including, when appropriate, compensation.

AHRQ's CANDOR process — Communication AND Optimal Resolution — is one of the most well-developed and widely referenced CRP frameworks available. It was built from the work of early adopters like the University of Michigan Health System, whose implementation of transparent communication and proactive resolution led to notable reductions in malpractice lawsuits and claims costs.

Key Concept

A CANDOR event is any event involving unexpected harm — physical, emotional, or financial — to a patient. The process activates even when a cause is not yet known. You don't wait for answers. You respond because a patient was harmed.

The Five Components of CANDOR

The CANDOR process is an integrated system with five components that work together. Components 2 through 5 operate as a cyclical process — investigation informs disclosure, which informs resolution, which generates organizational learning.

1
Identification of a CANDOR Event

An unexpected harm event is recognized and reported. This is the trigger that activates the entire process. The harm may be physical, emotional, or financial — and the cause does not need to be known.

2
CANDOR System Activation

The organization activates its multidisciplinary CANDOR Response Team. This coordinated activation triggers the next two components — Response/Disclosure and Investigation — to begin simultaneously.

3
Response and Disclosure

This component has two parallel tracks. Disclosure Communication begins with an initial conversation within 60 minutes of event identification — not a full explanation, but an honest acknowledgment that something unexpected happened. Full disclosure occurs after investigation, typically within 30–45 days. Care for the Caregiver provides structured support for the clinicians and staff impacted by the event — addressing the second victim phenomenon directly.

4
Event Investigation and Analysis

Running parallel to disclosure, a thorough system-focused investigation examines what happened, why, and what conditions contributed. This connects directly to the RCA and RCA2 methodologies you're studying this week. The goal: understand root causes and develop a plan to prevent recurrence.

5
Resolution

The organization applies what the investigation revealed to improve systems and prevent future harm. Resolution may also include determining how to address the harm event with the patient and family — which can include an offer of compensation. This is where learning translates to lasting change.

Connecting to Our Course Themes

Notice how CANDOR operationalizes concepts we've been building this term. Just Culture — separating system failures from reckless behavior and meeting harm with healing. Psychological Safety — providers must feel safe reporting events for the process to work. Systems Thinking — investigation focuses on system conditions, not just individual actions. Values-Based Leadership — truth through transparent disclosure, compassion through care for both patients and caregivers, justice through fair investigation and resolution.

Implementation: What Leaders Need to Know

Implementing a CRP like CANDOR is not a quick project — AHRQ estimates 12 to 18 months for full implementation. As future healthcare administrators, understanding the scope and leadership commitment required is essential. CANDOR implementation unfolds across three phases:

Phase I
Assessment
~3 months
Phase II
Process Implementation
~12 months
Phase III
Sustainability
~3 months + ongoing

Phase I: Assessment — Building the business case, securing organizational buy-in from senior leadership, conducting a gap analysis of current policies and processes, and assembling a multidisciplinary implementation team. This is "setting the stage."

Phase II: CANDOR Process Implementation — The longest phase. Training staff across the organization, operationalizing event reporting, disclosure communication protocols, Care for the Caregiver programs, and resolution processes. Data collection on process and outcome measures begins here.

Phase III: Sustainability — Ensuring the process becomes embedded in organizational culture. Continuous training, ongoing data collection, and alignment with the organization's mission and values. This is where high reliability principles intersect with CRP — sustainability requires ongoing commitment, not a one-time implementation.

Leadership Insight

The CANDOR Implementation Team Leader must be a senior leader with influence and authority to drive culture change. This cannot be delegated to middle management and forgotten. Successful implementation requires visible, committed leadership — the kind of leadership you are being prepared for in this program.
